Editorial Summary

The LLM RL landscape in mid-April 2026 is remarkably vibrant. Several dominant themes emerge from this week's harvest of 35 papers:

Policy optimization continues to diversify. We see a proliferation of GRPO variants — SKPO introduces skip connections for better early-token credit assignment, SPPO reformulates PPO at the sequence level for long-horizon reasoning, TEPO links group-level rewards to token-level updates, and StaRPO incorporates reasoning stability metrics. Meanwhile, TPO proposes a clean separation of "what to reinforce" from "how to update," and Policy Split explores dual-mode entropy regularization.

Training efficiency is a first-class concern. Two infrastructure papers stand out: Relax provides a fully asynchronous, omni-modal RL engine achieving 2x speedups, while experience replay studies show 40% compute savings from well-designed replay buffers. QaRL tackles the training-inference mismatch from quantized rollouts, and NExt accelerates RLVR via low-rank trajectory extrapolation.

RLVR (RL with Verifiable Rewards) matures. SUPERNOVA extends RLVR beyond math/code to general reasoning. SD-Zero converts binary rewards into dense supervision through self-revision. Multiple papers address exploration challenges in RLVR, including Cog-DRIFT's cognitive reformulation and bidirectional entropy modulation.

Reward hacking gets serious attention. SignCert-PO, robust optimization with correlated proxies, and DRTO all tackle reward hacking from different angles — certification, distributionally robust optimization, and token-level robustness respectively.

  • Most impactful: SD-Zero (2604.12002) for its elegant approach to sample-efficient RL, and Relax (2604.11554) for advancing multi-modal RL infrastructure
  • Most novel: SKPO (2604.08690) for its skip-connection insight on credit assignment, and Cog-DRIFT (2604.04767) for cognitive reformulation of hard problems
  • Best survey: Credit Assignment in LLM RL (2604.09459) — a comprehensive map of 47 methods across reasoning and agentic settings

Policy Optimization Methods — 8 papers

SKPO: Skip-Connected Policy Optimization for Implicit Advantage in LLM Reasoning
Fengwei Teng, Jinyi Bai, Xinhao Yao, et al. • 2604.08690 • April 11, 2026 • Method
GRPO is effective for LLM reasoning but struggles with credit assignment for early tokens under practical sampling budgets, where dense outcome rewards yield high-variance advantage estimates. SKPO addresses this by decomposing reasoning into upstream (early exploration) and downstream (solution convergence) phases connected by a skip connection. The upstream phase receives implicit advantage signals propagated backward from the downstream phase's verified outcomes, reducing variance for early-step tokens without requiring explicit process reward models.

Key Takeaways

  • Achieves 3.91% and 6.17% relative gains over baselines on Qwen2.5-Math-7B and Llama-3.2-3B respectively
  • Addresses a known weakness of GRPO: noisy advantage estimates for tokens early in the reasoning chain
  • No additional reward model required — exploits structural decomposition of the reasoning trajectory
Methodology: Splits CoT into two phases; downstream phase trained with standard GRPO, upstream phase receives skip-connected advantage signal. Evaluated on mathematical reasoning benchmarks.
SPPO: Sequence-Level PPO for Long-Horizon Reasoning Tasks
Tianyi Wang, Yixia Li, Long Li, et al. • 2604.08865 • April 11, 2026 • Method
Token-level PPO struggles with temporal credit assignment over long chain-of-thought (CoT) horizons — the value function must learn to evaluate partial reasoning sequences, which is notoriously difficult. SPPO reformulates LLM reasoning as a Sequence-Level Contextual Bandit problem, introducing a decoupled scalar value function that operates at the sequence level rather than the token level. This sidesteps the temporal credit assignment problem entirely while maintaining PPO's clipped objective for stable optimization.

Key Takeaways

  • Matches the performance of computation-heavy group-based methods (GRPO) while being significantly more resource-efficient
  • Eliminates the need for per-token value estimation, which is the primary bottleneck in token-level PPO for long sequences
  • Contextual bandit formulation is a clean theoretical framework for reasoning-as-generation
Methodology: Reformulates reasoning RL as contextual bandit; scalar value function scores complete sequences. Uses PPO clipping at the sequence level. Benchmarked on long-CoT reasoning tasks.
TEPO: Token-Level Policy Optimization — Linking Group-Level Rewards to Token-Level Aggregation
Xingyu Lin, Yilin Wen, Du Su, et al. (Jilin University, Baidu, Tsinghua) • 2604.12736 • April 14, 2026 • MethodNew
While GRPO assigns the same group-level advantage to all tokens in a response, individual tokens contribute differently to correctness. TEPO bridges this gap by leveraging sequence-level likelihood to create a principled link between group-level rewards and individual token contributions. It introduces a token-level KL-divergence mask constraint that selectively applies regularization, preventing the optimization from over-rotating on low-confidence tokens while still allowing high-confidence tokens to be reinforced strongly.

Key Takeaways

  • Addresses the "uniform credit" problem in GRPO — not all tokens deserve equal reinforcement
  • Token-level KL-divergence masking is a practical, low-overhead improvement over standard KL penalties
  • Strong results on CoT reasoning where sparse token-level rewards make standard GRPO suboptimal
Methodology: Uses sequence likelihood as proxy for token importance; applies masked KL constraint. Built on GRPO framework, evaluated on mathematical reasoning tasks.
Policy Split: Dual-Mode Exploration via Dual-Mode Entropy Regularization
Jiashu Yao, Heyan Huang, Chuwei Luo, et al. • 2604.11510 • April 13, 2026 • Method
Exploration-exploitation tradeoffs in LLM RL are typically managed by a single entropy regularization term, which creates a fundamental tension: high entropy for exploration degrades response quality, while low entropy for exploitation stifles discovery of novel reasoning paths. Policy Split resolves this by bifurcating the policy into two modes — a normal mode optimized for task correctness and a high-entropy mode (triggered by a special prompt) optimized for exploration. Both modes share model parameters but undergo collaborative dual-mode entropy regularization, allowing the model to explore aggressively without contaminating its task performance.

Key Takeaways

  • Elegant solution to the exploration-exploitation dilemma: separate the modes but share parameters
  • High-entropy mode acts as a controlled "brainstorming" phase that feeds discoveries to the normal mode
  • Applicable to any policy gradient method without architectural changes
Methodology: Dual-mode policy with shared parameters; high-entropy prompt triggers exploration mode. Collaborative regularization ensures modes benefit from each other.
TPO: Target Policy Optimization — Separating What to Reinforce from How to Update
Jean Kaddour • 2604.06159 • April 7, 2026 • Method
TPO makes a clean conceptual separation between two questions in RL for LLMs: (1) which completions should gain probability mass, and (2) how should the parameters move to achieve this. It constructs an explicit target distribution by mixing high-reward completions and then fits the policy to this target via cross-entropy minimization. This avoids the instabilities of policy gradient estimation while preserving the benefits of reward-weighted optimization. The framework subsumes several existing methods (policy gradient, PPO, GRPO, DPO) as special cases, providing a unifying perspective.

Key Takeaways

  • Provides a unifying theoretical framework: PG, PPO, GRPO, and DPO are all special cases of target policy optimization
  • Matches existing methods on easy tasks and substantially outperforms them under sparse reward settings
  • Clean, principled approach: construct the target distribution first, then distill into the policy
Methodology: Constructs target distribution from reward-weighted completions; policy fitted via cross-entropy. Theoretical analysis shows connections to PG/PPO/GRPO/DPO.
StaRPO: Stability-Augmented Reinforcement Policy Optimization
Jinghan Zhang, Fengran Mo, Tharindu Cyril Weerasooriya, et al. • 2604.08905 • April 11, 2026 • MethodReasoning
Standard RL objectives for reasoning LLMs optimize only for final-answer correctness, ignoring the quality and stability of the reasoning process itself. StaRPO incorporates reasoning stability as an explicit optimization signal by decomposing it into two measurable dimensions: Autocorrelation Function (ACF) for local step-to-step coherence, and Path Efficiency (PE) for global goal-directedness. These stability metrics are combined with task rewards to create process-aware feedback, encouraging the model to find not just correct but also stable and efficient reasoning paths.

Key Takeaways

  • First method to formalize and optimize "reasoning stability" in LLM RL
  • ACF and Path Efficiency are intuitive, interpretable metrics that capture coherence and directedness
  • Stable reasoning paths generalize better and are more interpretable to users
Methodology: Augments reward with stability metrics (ACF + PE). Process-aware feedback replaces pure outcome reward. Evaluated on multi-step reasoning benchmarks.
DDO-RM: Distributionally Guided Preference Optimization with Reward Models
Tiantian Zhang, Jierui Zuo, Wenping Wang • 2604.11119 • April 13, 2026 • MethodAlignment
DDO-RM bridges the gap between reward-model-based and reward-model-free preference optimization. It forms a policy distribution over candidate responses, centers reward-model scores under that distribution, and distills a reward-guided target distribution back into the policy. The approach provides a minimal held-out benchmark against DPO, showing that incorporating reward model information during optimization can improve over pure preference-based methods, particularly when the reward model captures aspects of quality that pairwise preferences miss.

Key Takeaways

  • Hybrid approach: combines strengths of reward-model-based and preference-based optimization
  • Centering reward scores under the policy distribution reduces reward hacking tendencies
  • Directly benchmarked against DPO, providing a useful comparison point
Methodology: Policy distribution over candidates; centered reward scores; target distribution distillation. Benchmarked against DPO on standard alignment tasks.
DRTO: Distributionally Robust Token Optimization in RLHF
Yeping Jin, Jiaming Hu, Ioannis Ch. Paschalidis • 2604.08577 • April 11, 2026 • MethodReward
Combines token-level RLHF with Distributionally Robust Optimization (DRO) to address the fragility of reward models at the token level. Standard token-level optimization can exploit spurious token-reward correlations; DRTO bounds worst-case token-wise rewards via an f-divergence ambiguity set. This ensures that the policy is optimized against the worst plausible token-level reward function within a neighborhood of the learned reward model, providing robustness guarantees against reward model inaccuracies at fine granularity.

Key Takeaways

  • 9.17% improvement on GSM8K and 2.49% on MathQA over standard token-level RLHF
  • DRO at the token level is a principled way to handle reward model uncertainty
  • f-divergence ambiguity sets provide tunable robustness without excessive conservatism
Methodology: Token-level RLHF with DRO using f-divergence ambiguity sets for worst-case reward bounds. Evaluated on math reasoning benchmarks.

RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) — 7 papers

Self-Distillation Zero: Self-Revision Turns Binary Rewards into Dense Supervision
Yinghui He, Simran Kaur, Adithya Bhaskar, et al. (Princeton) • 2604.12002 • April 13, 2026 • MethodNew
SD-Zero is a remarkably elegant approach to sample-efficient RL that requires no external teacher or high-quality demonstrations. A single model plays two roles: a Generator that produces an initial response, and a Reviser that conditions on that response plus its binary reward to produce an improved version. On-policy self-distillation then transfers the Reviser's knowledge back to the Generator — the Reviser's token distributions (conditioned on the Generator's response and reward) serve as dense, token-level supervision. In effect, the model learns to transform sparse binary rewards into rich self-supervision, dramatically improving sample efficiency over standard RL approaches.

Key Takeaways

  • Substantially more sample-efficient than standard RL — no need for large rollout budgets
  • No external teacher, reward model, or demonstration data required — fully self-contained
  • The Generator→Reviser→Distill loop is a general-purpose framework applicable beyond RLVR
  • Dense token-level supervision from binary rewards is a powerful conceptual insight
Methodology: Dual-role model (Generator + Reviser). Reviser conditions on response + binary reward. Self-distillation transfers token distributions from Reviser to Generator. Evaluated on math and code reasoning tasks.
SUPERNOVA: Eliciting General Reasoning in LLMs with Reinforcement Learning on Natural Instructions
Ashima Suvarna, Kendrick Phan, Mehrab Beikzadeh, et al. • 2604.08477 • April 11, 2026 • Reasoning
RLVR has achieved impressive gains in formal domains (math, code) where correctness is easily verified, but extending it to general reasoning — where ground truth is ambiguous and verification is harder — has remained an open challenge. SUPERNOVA addresses this with a data curation framework that identifies natural-language instructions amenable to verifiable reward signals. Models trained with SUPERNOVA outperform strong baselines including Qwen3.5 on BBEH (up to 52.8% relative improvement), Zebralogic, and MMLU-Pro, demonstrating that RLVR can be effectively extended to broad reasoning domains.

Key Takeaways

  • First demonstration that RLVR can match or exceed SFT-trained models on general (non-math) reasoning
  • 52.8% relative improvement on BBEH over strong baselines is a striking result
  • Data curation is the key insight: not all instructions are suitable for RLVR, and systematic selection matters
Methodology: Data curation framework filters instructions suitable for verifiable reward. RLVR training with curated data. Evaluated on BBEH, Zebralogic, MMLU-Pro.
An Imperfect Verifier is Good Enough: Learning with Noisy Rewards in RLVR
Andreas Plesner, Francisco Guzman, Anish Athalye • 2604.07666 • April 10, 2026 • Reasoning
A critical practical question for RLVR: how noisy can the verifier be before training breaks down? This paper provides a reassuring answer — noise rates up to 15% yield peak validation accuracy within 2 percentage points of clean baselines. The finding is consistent across three model families (Qwen3, GLM4, Llama 3.1) at sizes 4B–9B, and across code generation and scientific reasoning tasks. This suggests that perfect verifiers are unnecessary for effective RLVR, significantly lowering the bar for applying RLVR to new domains where verifiers may be approximate.

Key Takeaways

  • RLVR is robust to 15% verifier noise — a reassuring result for practical deployment
  • Consistent across 3 model families and 2 task types, suggesting generalizability
  • Lowers the barrier for RLVR in new domains: approximate verifiers are sufficient
Methodology: Systematic noise injection at varying rates (0–40%) into verifiers for RLVR. Evaluated across Qwen3-4B/9B, GLM4-9B, Llama-3.1-8B on code generation and scientific reasoning.
Rethinking Exploration in RLVR: Bidirectional Entropy Modulation
Hengrui Gu, Xiaotian Han, Yujing Bian, Kaixiong Zhou • 2604.04894 • April 7, 2026 • Method
Addresses the "restricted exploration" problem in RLVR where the policy rapidly converges to a narrow set of solutions. Standard entropy regularization is unreliable for LLMs because it conflates two types of entropy: informative entropy (diverse solution paths) and spurious entropy (eroding reasoning patterns). The paper decomposes policy entropy along these axes and shows that group-relative advantage estimation (as in GRPO) implicitly acts as an entropy refinement mechanism, selectively preserving informative entropy while reducing spurious entropy. This insight motivates a bidirectional entropy modulation technique.

Key Takeaways

  • Informative vs. spurious entropy decomposition is a key conceptual contribution
  • Reveals that GRPO's advantage estimation implicitly refines entropy — explains part of its success
  • Bidirectional modulation: boost informative entropy, suppress spurious entropy
Methodology: Entropy decomposition analysis; bidirectional modulation technique. Evaluated on math reasoning with GRPO as base algorithm.
Cog-DRIFT: Exploration via Adaptively Reformulated Instances
Justin Chih-Yao Chen, Archiki Prasad, Zaid Khan, et al. (UNC Chapel Hill) • 2604.04767 • April 7, 2026 • Reasoning
Models cannot learn from problems they cannot solve — if the current policy gets zero reward on a problem, there is no gradient signal. Cog-DRIFT addresses this "exploration barrier" by transforming hard open-ended problems into cognitively simpler variants (multiple-choice, cloze formats) that preserve the original answer while reducing the effective search space. This creates a curriculum of decreasing cognitive assistance, allowing the model to gradually tackle harder problem formats as its capabilities improve. The approach is inspired by cognitive science research on how problem framing affects solving difficulty.

Key Takeaways

  • Novel cognitive-science-inspired approach: reformulate hard problems, don't just filter them out
  • Adaptive curriculum from guided (multiple-choice) to open-ended formats
  • Directly addresses the exploration barrier that limits RLVR on challenging problems
Methodology: Problem reformulation pipeline (open→MC→cloze). Adaptive curriculum based on policy success rate. Evaluated on hard math and reasoning problems.
Self-Distilled RLVR: Token-Level Self-Distillation for Verifiable Reward Training
Chenxu Yang, Chuanyu Qin, Qingyi Si, et al. • 2604.03128 • April 4, 2026 • Method
RLSD combines self-distillation with RLVR, using token-level policy differences between current and previous checkpoints to determine fine-grained update magnitudes. While RLVR provides reliable update directions (which tokens to reinforce), self-distillation provides better-calibrated magnitudes (how much to reinforce each token). This combination achieves a higher convergence ceiling and superior training stability compared to either RLVR or on-policy self-distillation alone, suggesting the two techniques are complementary rather than competing.

Key Takeaways

  • RLVR for direction + self-distillation for magnitude is a principled combination
  • Higher convergence ceiling than either method alone
  • Token-level policy differences between checkpoints provide meaningful fine-grained signal
Methodology: Token-level KL between current and previous policy as update magnitude; RLVR outcome as update direction. Evaluated on math reasoning.
PubSwap: Public-Data Off-Policy Coordination for Federated RLVR
Anupam Nayak, Baris Askin, Muhammed Ustaomeroglu, et al. (CMU) • 2604.12160 • April 13, 2026 • Infrastructure
Scales RLVR to federated settings where training data is distributed across clients and cannot be centralized due to privacy constraints. PubSwap combines LoRA-based local adaptation (each client fine-tunes on private data) with public-data off-policy steps for cross-client coordination and communication efficiency. The off-policy public-data steps serve as a synchronization mechanism, ensuring that federated models don't diverge excessively while still benefiting from local private data. This is the first work to address the intersection of federated learning and RLVR.

Key Takeaways

  • First work on federated RLVR — an important direction for privacy-preserving LLM training
  • LoRA for local adaptation + public data for coordination is a practical architecture
  • Off-policy public-data steps as a synchronization mechanism is a novel contribution
Methodology: Federated LoRA training with public-data off-policy coordination. Communication-efficient protocol for cross-client synchronization.

Reward Modeling & Reward Hacking — 5 papers

Multi-response Reward Modeling in a Single Forward Pass
Authors not fully available • 2604.10966 • April 13, 2026 • RewardInfrastructure
Standard reward models evaluate each candidate response independently, requiring N forward passes to score N candidates. This paper proposes a Multi-Response discriminative reward model that concatenates all N candidate responses into a single input sequence and uses a multi-response scoring head to produce scores for all responses simultaneously. The approach exploits cross-response attention patterns to produce better-calibrated relative scores while dramatically reducing inference cost. At N=4 candidates, the method achieves up to 3.9x latency reduction and 4.0x FLOPs reduction with comparable or better reward quality.

Key Takeaways

  • 3.9x latency and 4.0x FLOPs reduction at N=4 candidates — directly impacts RLHF training throughput
  • Cross-response attention enables better relative scoring compared to independent evaluation
  • Directly pluggable into GRPO/Best-of-N pipelines that already generate multiple candidates
Methodology: Concatenates N responses into single sequence; multi-head scoring architecture. Evaluated on reward model benchmarks and downstream RLHF training quality.
SignCert-PO: Mitigating Reward Hacking via Advantage Sign Robustness
Shinnosuke Ono, Johannes Ackermann, Soichiro Nishimori, et al. • 2604.02986 • April 3, 2026 • Reward
Reward hacking in RLHF often arises from flipped advantage signs — the reward model incorrectly ranks a worse response above a better one, causing the policy to reinforce the wrong behavior. SignCert-PO derives a certified sign-preservation radius: a mathematical guarantee that the advantage sign is correct within a certain perturbation ball around the reward model's estimate. Completions whose advantages fall outside this certified radius are down-weighted during optimization. This is a lightweight, purely optimization-stage intervention that requires no changes to the reward model itself.

Key Takeaways

  • Certified robustness for advantage signs — a formal guarantee against a specific failure mode
  • Lightweight: operates purely at the policy optimization stage, no reward model changes needed
  • Down-weighting non-robust completions is more principled than uniform clipping
Methodology: Derives certified sign-preservation radius from reward model uncertainty. Down-weights completions outside certified radius. Compatible with any PG-based method.
Robust Optimization for Mitigating Reward Hacking with Correlated Proxies
Zixuan Liu, Xiaolin Sun, Zizhan Zheng • 2604.12086 • April 13, 2026 • Reward
Formulates reward hacking as a robust policy optimization problem over all r-correlated proxy rewards — the set of all reward functions that agree with the proxy reward model to a specified degree. This formulation captures the fundamental uncertainty in reward modeling: we know the proxy is correlated with the true reward, but we don't know exactly how. The paper derives a tractable max-min formulation that can be optimized efficiently, providing worst-case guarantees against reward model exploitation while not being overly conservative.

Key Takeaways

  • Elegant formulation: optimize against all r-correlated proxy rewards simultaneously
  • Tractable max-min formulation — theoretical rigor with practical applicability
  • Provides worst-case guarantees without excessive conservatism
Methodology: Robust optimization over r-correlated proxy reward set. Derives tractable max-min formulation. Evaluated on standard RLHF benchmarks.
Plan-RewardBench: Trajectory-Level Reward Modeling for Agentic Systems
Authors (see paper) • 2604.08178 • April 11, 2026 • RewardAgentic
As LLMs evolve into agentic systems with multi-step tool use, reward models must evaluate entire trajectories rather than single responses. Plan-RewardBench is a trajectory-level preference benchmark for evaluating how well reward models and LLM judges can distinguish preferred agent trajectories in complex tool-using scenarios. The benchmark covers planning, execution, error recovery, and tool selection across diverse agentic tasks, providing the first systematic evaluation of reward models in the agentic setting.

Key Takeaways

  • First benchmark specifically designed for trajectory-level reward modeling in agentic systems
  • Covers planning, execution, error recovery — all critical dimensions for agent evaluation
  • Reveals significant gaps in current reward models' ability to judge agent trajectories
Methodology: Curated trajectory pairs across diverse agentic tasks with human preference annotations. Evaluates reward models and LLM judges on trajectory-level preference prediction.
Uncertainty-Aware Variational Reward Factorization for LLM Personalization
Gyuseok Lee, Wonbin Kweon, Zhenrui Yue, et al. (UIUC) • 2604.00997 • April 1, 2026 • RewardAlignment
Standard reward models learn a single preference function, but real users have diverse, often conflicting preferences. VRF represents each user's preferences as a variational distribution in a shared preference basis space, with variance-attenuated loss to handle uncertainty in individual user preferences. The factorized representation enables efficient personalization: a new user's reward function can be approximated from a small number of preference judgments by projecting onto the learned basis, without retraining the reward model.

Key Takeaways

  • Variational factorization enables per-user reward models without per-user training
  • Variance attenuation handles noisy individual preferences gracefully
  • Shared preference basis captures common structure across users while allowing personalization
Methodology: Variational reward factorization into user-specific weights over shared preference bases. Variance-attenuated loss for robust training. Evaluated on personalized alignment tasks.

Training Infrastructure & Efficiency — 5 papers

Relax: An Asynchronous Reinforcement Learning Engine for Omni-Modal Post-Training at Scale
Liujie Zhang, Benzhe Ning, Rui Yang, et al. (Xiaohongshu, HKU, USTC) • 2604.11554 • April 13, 2026 • InfrastructureNew
As RL training extends to omni-modal inputs (image, text, audio, video) and agentic multi-turn workflows, existing systems face three interdependent challenges: heterogeneous data flows, operational robustness at scale, and the staleness-throughput tradeoff. Relax addresses all three with an architecture where each RL role runs as an independent, fault-isolated service connected via a TransferQueue data bus. A single staleness parameter smoothly interpolates among on-policy, near-on-policy, and fully asynchronous execution. The omni-native design builds multimodal support into the full stack rather than retrofitting it onto a text-centric pipeline. Relax achieves 1.76x speedup on Qwen3-4B and 2.00x on Qwen3-Omni-30B in fully async mode, and demonstrates stable omni-modal RL convergence across modalities including 2000+ training steps on video.

Key Takeaways

  • 2x speedup over synchronous baselines with fully async execution — critical for large-scale training
  • First open-source RL engine with native multimodal support across text, image, audio, and video
  • Fault-isolated services enable independent scaling and recovery — production-grade robustness
  • Staleness-throughput tradeoff is explicitly parametrized rather than binary
Methodology: Service-oriented architecture with TransferQueue data bus. Configurable staleness parameter. Benchmarked on Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-Omni-30B across modalities.
Efficient RL Training for LLMs with Experience Replay
Charles Arnal, Vivien Cabannes, Taco Cohen, Julia Kempe, Remi Munos • 2604.08706 • April 9, 2026 • Infrastructure
Challenges the conventional wisdom that strict on-policy sampling is essential for LLM RL training. Through a systematic study, the paper formalizes the optimal design of replay buffers as a three-way tradeoff between staleness-induced variance, sample diversity, and computational cost. The key finding: when generation is expensive (as it is for large LLMs), a well-designed replay buffer that reuses slightly stale samples can save up to 40% of the compute budget while maintaining — and sometimes surpassing — the final accuracy of purely on-policy methods. This is because sample diversity from the buffer compensates for staleness.

Key Takeaways

  • 40% compute savings from well-designed replay buffers — significant practical impact
  • Staleness-diversity tradeoff is formalized: diversity gains can outweigh staleness costs
  • Simple, easy-to-implement buffer strategies — no complex architectures required
Methodology: Systematic study of replay buffer designs varying retention policy, refresh rate, and mixing ratio. Evaluated on LLM reasoning tasks with compute-matched comparisons.
NExt: Low-Rank Optimization Trajectories Modeling for LLM RLVR Acceleration
Zhipeng Chen, Tao Qian, Wayne Xin Zhao, Ji-Rong Wen • 2604.11446 • April 13, 2026 • Infrastructure
RLVR requires extensive exploration and learning, leading to substantial computational overhead. NExt observes that while the rank-1 subspace of model parameters evolves non-linearly during RLVR training, this evolution is predictable. It models and extrapolates low-rank parameter trajectories during training, effectively predicting where the model would be after several more training steps. The dominance of low-rank structure is further amplified during LoRA training. By extrapolating rather than computing these updates, NExt reduces computational overhead by approximately 37.5% while remaining compatible with various RLVR algorithms.

Key Takeaways

  • 37.5% compute reduction via trajectory extrapolation — orthogonal to other efficiency methods
  • Low-rank trajectory structure is a fundamental property of RLVR training, not an approximation
  • Compatible with any RLVR algorithm — a general-purpose acceleration technique
Methodology: Models rank-1 subspace evolution during RLVR training; uses nonlinear extrapolation to predict future parameters. Compatible with LoRA and full-parameter training.
QaRL: Rollout-Aligned Quantization-Aware RL for Fast and Stable Training
Hao Gu, Hao Wang, Jiacheng Liu, et al. (HKUST, CityU, ZJU) • 2604.07853 • April 10, 2026 • Infrastructure
Using quantized models for rollout generation (to reduce inference cost) introduces a training-inference mismatch that can destabilize RL training. QaRL identifies and addresses a specific failure mode: repetitive garbled tokens that emerge when the quantized rollout model diverges from the full-precision training model. The paper proposes TBPO (Trust-Band Policy Optimization), a sequence-level objective with dual clipping that constrains the update magnitude for both positive and negative samples. This prevents the training model from chasing artifacts of quantization while still benefiting from the computational savings of quantized rollouts.

Key Takeaways

  • Identifies a concrete failure mode of quantized rollouts: repetitive garbled tokens
  • TBPO's dual clipping prevents the training model from over-fitting to quantization artifacts
  • Enables practical use of quantized inference for rollouts, reducing compute costs significantly
Methodology: Trust-Band Policy Optimization with dual clipping at the sequence level. Evaluated with FP4/INT4 quantized rollout models against full-precision baselines.
I-PPO: Learning from the Right Rollouts via Influence-Guided PPO
Dong Shu, Denghui Zhang, Jessica Hullman • 2604.01597 • April 2, 2026 • MethodInfrastructure
Not all RL training episodes are equally useful — some may even be harmful by encouraging unfaithful chain-of-thought reasoning. I-PPO integrates data attribution into the RL post-training loop, computing influence scores for each rollout episode based on its alignment with a validation gradient. Episodes that are anti-aligned (potentially harmful to downstream performance) are identified and eliminated before they influence the policy update. This acts as an intrinsic, data-driven early stopping mechanism that reduces unfaithful CoT reasoning while improving sample efficiency.

Key Takeaways

  • Data attribution in RL training — identifies and removes harmful training episodes
  • Reduces unfaithful chain-of-thought reasoning — a quality issue beyond mere accuracy
  • Acts as intrinsic early stopping without manually tuned stopping criteria
Methodology: Influence functions compute per-episode alignment with validation gradient. Anti-aligned episodes filtered before policy update. Built on PPO framework.

Agentic RL — 5 papers

DeepReinforce Team • 2604.02721 • April 3, 2026 • AgenticNew
A multi-agent RL system that represents a landmark achievement in competitive programming. GrandCode introduces Agentic GRPO — extending GRPO to handle multi-stage agent rollouts with delayed rewards across planning, coding, debugging, and testing phases. The key innovation is treating competitive programming as a multi-turn agentic task rather than single-shot code generation, with RL rewards delayed until final test execution.

Key Takeaways

  • First AI system to beat all humans in live Codeforces contests — a concrete milestone
  • Agentic GRPO: extends GRPO to multi-stage rollouts with delayed rewards
  • Multi-agent architecture for planning, coding, debugging, testing — mirrors human workflow
  • Demonstrates that agentic RL can surpass single-shot generation in complex tasks
Methodology: Agentic GRPO with multi-stage rollouts (plan→code→debug→test). Delayed reward from final test execution. Multi-agent architecture with specialized roles. Evaluated on live Codeforces contests.
RefineRL: Self-Refinement Reinforcement Learning for Competitive Programming
Shaopeng Fu, Xingxing Zhang, Li Dong, Di Wang, Furu Wei (Microsoft Research) • 2604.00790 • April 1, 2026 • Agentic
Introduces a Skeptical-Agent with local execution tools for iterative self-refinement in competitive programming, combined with RL using standard RLVR data. The agent generates initial code, tests it locally, identifies failures, and iteratively refines — all trained end-to-end with RL.
